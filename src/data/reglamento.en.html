Closing Notes

Core Concepts

Before starting to play, it is important to clarify some basic concepts that are used throughout the entire regulation. ZeroLore uses simple and direct language, but these definitions avoid confusion during the game.

Miniatures, units and squads

one miniature represents an individual combatant, creature, or vehicle on the battlefield.

one unit It is the basic activation element of the game.

  • In skirmish mode, each miniature acts as an independent unit.
  • In squad mode, a unit represents an organized group of several miniatures that act as a single entity.

one square It is a unit made up of several miniatures that share activation, actions and objectives. Although the models may carry different weapons, they are all part of the same unit for the purposes of the rules.

Consistency and placement of squads

In squad mode, all models that are part of a squad must stay within range. 1” or less of at least 2 miniatures from the same squad or one if it is made up of 2 units, forming a coherent group.

Thumbnail placement only affects which models can attack in close combat and does not modify the number of actions, status or activation of the squad.

(suggested image: individual miniature vs. squad of several miniatures)

Dice and rolls

ZeroLore uses six-sided dice (1D) and sometimes direction dice (1Direction)

  • Each roll is resolved by rolling one or more dice as indicated by the rule.
  • A critical hit is normally obtained with a result of 6, unless a rule indicates otherwise.
  • The results of 1 or 2 They are often considered failures in melee combat.
  • When a rule allows repeat a roll, each die can only be repeated once.

If multiple rules allow a reroll, only one reroll applies.

(suggested image: given marking 6 as critical)

Rule Priority

If a special rule, skill, doctrine, or profile contradicts a general rule of the rulebook, special rule takes priority.

The rules that come from:

  • unit skills,
  • weapon skills,
  • command doctrines,
  • faction rules,

They always take precedence over the basic rules, unless explicitly stated otherwise.

Game Modes

ZeroLore can be played in two main modes: Skirmish and Squads.

The game mode is chosen before starting the game, and all players must use the same mode.

  • Skirmish Mode- Each miniature acts as an independent unit. It is a more tactical and narrative mode, ideal for quick games and small tables.
  • Squads Mode- Units are grouped into squads that act as a single entity. It is a mode designed for larger scale battles.

Unless otherwise noted, all rules in the regulations apply to both modes.

Distance Measurement

All distances are measured in inches (").

  • Measurements are made from the closest point of the base of the unit acting to the nearest point of the objective.
  • Distances can be measured at any time during the game.
  • In case of doubt, any of the players can request a precise re-measurement, and the result is applied definitively.

(suggested image: measurement from base to base with ruler)

Line of sight

For a unit to ranged attack another, it must have line of sight.

  • A unit is considered to have line of sight if any part of the target thumbnail can be seen from the attacker's point of view.
  • The units have vision in 360 degrees; The orientation of the miniature does not limit its field of view.
  • If a model (friendly or enemy) or a piece of scenery completely blocks vision, the target cannot be attacked at the distance.

Cover and height rules are detailed later in the regulations.

(suggested image: example of clear and blocked line of sight)

The golden rule

ZeroLore is designed to be flexible. If a situation not covered by the rules arises during a game:

  • Players must solve it using the common sense and mutual agreement.
  • If there is no consensus, a 1D6 roll can be made to quickly decide and continue the game.

Turn Structure

A ZeroLore game is divided into shifts.

Each turn always follows the same structure and is resolved in an orderly manner to ensure a clear and balanced flow.

Each turn is made up of the following phases:

  1. Initiative Phase
  1. Activations Phase
  1. End of Turn

(suggested image: simple shift diagram with three blocks)

1. Initiative Phase

At the beginning of each turn, both players perform the Initiative roll.

  • Each player rolls 1D6.
  • The player with the highest score act first during that turn.
  • In case of a tie, the roll is repeated.

In team games, players on the same side can take a short time to coordinate their strategy before continuing.

Doctrines and stratagems

After determining the Initiative, the players declare the Command Doctrines or stratagems that they are going to use during that turn, following the rules of each doctrine.

  • The doctrines are activated in this phase.
  • Its effects last the whole turn, unless otherwise indicated.
  • Once a doctrine is used, can't be used again during the game.

(suggested image: doctrine marker or card activated)

2. Activations Phase

After the Initiative Phase begins Activations Phase.

  • The player who won the Initiative activates first.
  • Players alternate activations, one unit at a time.

Activating a unit means:

  • Declare it as the active unit.
  • Perform available actions according to its rules.
  • Completely resolve its effects.

Once a unit has been activated, cannot be activated again during that turn.

(suggested image: unit marker activated)

3. End of Turn

When all units of both players have been activated, the turn ends.

During the End of Turn:

  • Effects that indicate resolution at the end of the turn end.
  • The points corresponding to the game mode are counted.

After this, a new turn begins, starting again with the Initiative Phase.

Unit Actions

Each time a unit is activated during the Activations Phase, it can perform up to two actions.

  • The actions cannot be repeated during the same activation, unless a rule indicates otherwise.
  • The order in which the actions are carried out is free.
  • If a unit cannot or will not use an action, it is lost.

The actions available for all units are as follows:

  • Move – 1 action
  • Attack from a distance – 1 action
  • Prepared – 2 action
  • Retreat – 2 actions
  • Charge – 2 actions
  • Attack melee – 2 actions
  • Dash – 2 actions

(suggested image: simple icons representing each action)

Movement

When a unit moves, it can move up to its value. Movement in inches (").

  • Units can move in any direction.
  • You cannot move a unit closer than 1” from an enemy unit, except when charging.
  • Movement can be used to go around obstacles or position yourself freely on the battlefield.

Terrain and obstacles

A unit can:

  • Go around obstacles freely.
  • Climb elevated surfaces if your movement allows you to physically reach the desired height.

To climb:

  1. The unit must move until it touches the base of the obstacle with its base.
  1. Next, you measure the vertical height you want to scale, consuming movement.

The light coverage (windows, low barricades, small obstacles) can be crossed with normal movement as long as the unit is higher than the obstacle.

(suggested image: unit climbing an obstacle and crossing a barricade)

Dash

When making a Dash, the unit consumes 2 actions.

  • The unit pulls 1D6.
  • The result is added to your Movement value for that move.
  • During a Race no attacks can be made, unless a rule indicates otherwise.

The Run represents a fast and exposed movement, useful for repositioning or reaching cover.

Withdrawal

One unit can withdraw at any time, even if engaged in close combat.

To make a withdrawal:

  • The unit consumes 2 actions.
  • It moves using its Movement value in the direction of its deployment area, turning towards her.
  • Upon entering retreat, the unit reduces its Speed and Value at 0.

At the start of its next turn, the unit must roll 1D6:

  • 1–2: continues to retreat towards its deployment area.
  • 3–6: Recomposes himself and can act normally.

The units are automatically recompose the moment they reach their deployment zone.

(suggested image: retreat arrow towards deployment area)

Prepared

A unit that uses action Prepared adopts a defensive posture.

  • A prepared unit can perform ranged counterattacks during the opposing player's turn.
  • If fired, you can immediately return a shooting roll with the equipped weapon, regardless of whether you have any actions left.
  • If a unit charges against a prepared unit, the combat is resolved according to the rules of Speed:
    • If the ready unit has higher Speed, it will attack first.
    • The charging unit will perform the counterattack.

The Ready state lasts until the turn ends.

(suggested image: unit in defensive position aiming)

General considerations

  • If a player forgets to perform an action, use an ability, or declare an effect, it is considered lost, unless agreed between players.
  • It is recommended that miniatures carry the weapons represented in their profile to make the battlefield easier to read.
  • Communicating the intentions of each play helps keep the game fluid and clear.

Ranged combat

Units can perform ranged attacks against valid targets using the weapons in your arsenal.

To attack from a distance, the unit must:

  • have line of sight with the objective.
  • Be inside the scope of the weapon used.
  • Not be restricted by a special rule.

Ranged Attack Sequence

Every ranged attack is always resolved following this sequence:

  1. Choose target
  1. Determine attacks
  1. roll hits
  1. Roll saves
  1. Apply damage

(suggested image: numbered diagram of the shooting sequence)

1. Choose target

The attacking unit chooses an enemy unit visible and within weapon range.

  • A unit can attack only a target, unless a rule indicates otherwise.
  • If at least one model of the attacking unit has line of sight, the unit can make the attack.

2. Determine attacks

Each weapon indicates how many Attacks perform.

  • Roll as many dice as the weapon's Attack value indicates.
  • Each die represents an independent shot or projectile.

3. Hit roll

For each dice rolled:

  • If the result is equal to or greater than the Hit value of the weapon, the attack hits.
  • A result of 6 is considered a critical hit, unless a rule indicates otherwise.
  • Results lower than the Hit value are considered failures and are discarded.

The dice that impact are not rolled again.

(suggested image: example of dice with hits and crits)

4. Saving Roll

The defending player rolls as many dice as the unit has received hits.

  • Each result equal to or greater than the Save value blocks an impact.
  • Just one roll of 6 can block a critical hit.
  • Unblocked hits now deal damage.

5. Apply damage

Each unblocked hit deals the damage indicated by the weapon.

  • Normal hits inflict base damage.
  • Critical hits inflict critical damage of the weapon, if you have one.

When a unit loses all its Lives, it withdraws from the game as destroyed.

Melee Combat

Melee Combat occurs when two units meet in base-to-base contact. At that point, both units are locked in combat.

Charge

To initiate close combat, a unit must take the action Charge, which consumes 2 actions.

  • The unit rolls 2D6.
  • The result is the total distance you can travel toward the target, regardless of your Movement value.
  • If the unit does not reach the target, the charge fails and the unit remains in position.
  • If the charge is successful, the unit places its base in contact with the target and is considered locked.

A successful charge automatically includes a melee attack.

(suggested image: unit making a charge with arrow and distance 2D6)

Engaged units

When two units are in base-to-base contact:

  • They are considered locked in melee combat.
  • Both units can only use melee combat or withdrawal.
  • They remain locked until one of the two is eliminated or withdraws.

To unlock:

  • Eliminate the enemy unit, or
  • Declare a withdrawal following the withdrawal rules.

Attack order

The order in which units attack is determined as follows:

  1. If a unit has loaded, attack first.
  1. If none have charged, attack the unit first with higher speed.
  1. If both have the same Speed, the players roll 1D6 to decide.

This order applies every time an attack exchange is resolved.

Hand-to-hand combat resolution

Hand-to-hand combat is resolved following this sequence:

  1. First Acting Unit Attack
  1. Defender Saves
  1. Counterattack (if applicable)

(suggested image: simple melee attack sharing diagram)

1. Attack

The attacking unit rolls the dice Attacks indicated in its melee profile.

  • The results of 1 and 2 They are considered failures.
  • All other results are considered hits.
  • A result of 6 is a critical hit, unless a rule indicates otherwise.

2. Saves

The defending player rolls as many dice as there are hits he has received.

  • Each result equal to or greater than his Save value blocks an impact.
  • Critical hits can only be blocked with a roll of 6.
  • Unblocked hits deal the base or critical damage of the attacking weapon.

3. Counterattack

If the defending unit survives and has enough actions, you must carry out a mandatory counterattack.

  • The counterattack is resolved exactly the same as a melee attack.
  • If the unit is destroyed during the first attack, can't fight back.
  • If the unit has no actions available, it cannot perform the counterattack.

After the swap, both units remain locked.

Vehicles and monsters in close combat

  • If a vehicle is charged by a unit Line or Elite, you can continue moving and acting normally.
  • If loaded by another vehicle or by a monster, is locked in combat.
  • A locked vehicle can only use its melee weapons.

Monsters follow the same general rules of melee combat, except for their special rules.

Titans in melee combat

  • A Titan can only charge another Titan.
  • Line, Elite, Vehicle and Monster units they cannot charge or engage to a Titan by themselves.

Titans follow the same general rules of melee combat, except for their special rules.

Special squad rules

Use of weapons in squads

In squad mode, each model uses the weapon they have equipped.

If a squad has weapons with different profiles, attacks are resolved by weapon groups.

For each weapon group:

  1. All of his attacks (hits, saves, and damage) are resolved.
  1. Once resolved, move on to the next weapon type.

All weapons in the squad must attack the same target, unless a rule indicates otherwise.

The player controlling the squad decides the order in which the different types of weapons are resolved.

Melee combat in squads

Targeting in melee combat

In squad mode, although a squad may be in contact with several enemy squads, when making a melee attack you must choose a single target squad. If a squad is charged by several enemy units, it will be able to choose (if it has higher Speed) which one to attack when appropriate, following the normal rules of combat order.

If a unit of the squad comes into base-to-base contact with another unit, the entire squad is considered engaged, therefore, all units attack.

(suggested image: squad with miniatures in contact and others outside)

The attack action is considered consumed by the entire squad.

Cover

Covers modify the defense of attacked units:

Partial cover

  • The unit gets +1 save die against ranged attacks.
  • It can be charged and combat hand-to-hand.
  • In close combat, both units retreat -1 attack die in their rolls, unless a rule indicates otherwise.

Elevated cover

  • If the unit is located 2” or more in height, you get:
    • +1 save die.
    • +1 to Hits value of his ranged attacks.
  • If both units are at the same height, within 1” of each other, this rule does not apply.

Full cover

  • If no part of the target thumbnail is visible, cannot be attacked at range or in melee, except for special rules.
  • Models (friendly or enemy) that completely block vision count as full cover.

(suggested image: examples of partial, height and full coverage)

Shooting at Engaged Units

A unit engaged in close combat cannot be attacked from a distance, unless an ability indicates otherwise.

Unit States

During the game, some units may be in special states that modify their normal behavior.

These states represent specific tactical situations and have clear entry and exit rules.

The main states in ZeroLore are:

  • Prepared
  • Withdrawal
  • Loss of control
  • Locked

Ready Unit

A unit enters state Prepared when you use the action Prepared during its activation. (explained in the section: unit actions)

Retreating Unit

A unit enters Withdrawal when you declare the action Retreat.

(explained in the section: unit actions)

Engaged units

Two units are considered locked when they are in base-to-base contact.

(explained in the section: Hand-to-hand combat)

Uncontrolled unit

Some units, such as Monsters, they can enter a state of Loss of control when they suffer serious damage.

While uncontrolled:

  • The unit will attack both enemies as well as allied units.
  • He will always prioritize attacking the nearest unit.
  • You will only use melee attacks, prioritizing loads if possible.

The state of Out of Control is maintained until a rule indicates otherwise or the game ends.

(suggested image: unleashed creature attacking indiscriminately)

Battlefield control

Control of the battlefield is based on the occupation of Command points and key areas of the stage.

Command points

Command points function as strategic areas.

  • A unit that controls a command point and is not engaged in combat contributes +5 victory points at the end of each turn.
  • If there are multiple units in a command point, the position belongs to the player with the highest sum of the stat value of his units.
  • Command points are the basic victory condition in most scenarios.

If both players control the same command point, it is considered neutral.

(suggested image: command point marker with units contesting it)

Deployment area

The deployment zone represents the area where units begin the game or reorganize.

The deployment areas may vary depending on the type of game available to the players. But as a general rule they start at the ends of the table where the game is played.

In skirmish game mode the deployment zone would be 5" from the end of the table where the game is played. In squad game mode it would increase to 10".

There are situations in which units will have special deployment rules, indicated on their sheet.

In some game modes or scenarios:

  • Command points or deployment zones may allow recover lives if they are controlled.
  • In case of healing, a unit can recover up to a maximum of 3 Life at the end of the turn, unless otherwise indicated.

Unit Types

All ZeroLore units belong to a type.

The unit type defines its role on the battlefield, as well as what you can or cannot do within the game.

The type does not limit statistics of the unit, which depend on its faction and design, but does determine its interaction with objectives, special rules and scoring.

All units have, at a minimum, a melee attack in his arsenal.

1.Line Units

The units of Line They represent versatile or offensive troops, and constitute the core of most armies.

Features:

  • They are the bulk of the army.
  • They can capture and control Command points and other areas of conquest.
  • They tend to have balanced profiles and access to a wide variety of weapons.

(suggested image: basic infantry advancing and occupying an objective)

2.Elite Units

The units of Elite They are more resistant and powerful troops than those of Línea.

Features:

  • Greater offensive or defensive capacity.
  • Designed to break enemy formations or hold key positions.
  • They can capture and control Command points, same as Line units.

(suggested image: elite unit standing out for armor or heavy weaponry)

3.Vehicles

The Vehicles They represent war machines of great power and high cost.

Features:

  • Great offensive capacity or resistance.
  • They cannot climb structures or climb high obstacles.
  • Cannot capture or control Command points nor other areas of conquest.

When a vehicle is destroyed:

  • It produces a explosion which affects all units 3”.
  • Affected units receive 1D6 automatic damage, which cannot be saved.

(suggested image: damaged vehicle exploding and affecting nearby units)

4.Monsters

The Monsters They are creatures of enormous power and presence on the battlefield.

Features:

  • Very resistant and lethal in melee combat.
  • Like vehicles, They cannot capture or control Command points unless a rule says otherwise..

Loss of control in monsters

When a monster comes to 50% of their lives, the player must shoot 1D6 only once:

  • 3–6: The monster suffers no additional effects.
  • 1–2: the monster enters a state of Loss of control.

While uncontrolled:

  • Attack enemies and friendly units.
  • Always prioritize the closest unit.
  • Only use melee attacks, prioritizing charges.

(suggested image: out of control monster attacking indiscriminately)

5.Heroes

The Heroes They represent exceptional combatants and key figures within each faction.

General characteristics:

  • They are singular units that act as a single miniature.
  • Each player can include a single hero on your list unless a rule says otherwise.
  • All of its rules and abilities are described on its sheet.
  • The game is fully functional even without heroes.

Special Hero Rules:

  • They automatically control the Command points if they are present.
  • If both players have a hero at the same command point, they remain neutral.
  • They have unique abilities that define their tactical role.
  • In squad mode, some heroes can join specific squads According to their card, if they are part of a squad their: value, movement and speed replace that of the squad they are part of.

(suggested image: featured hero leading other units)

6. Titans

A Titan It is a colossal unit that represents war machines or gigantic creatures whose presence dominates the battlefield.

Titans do not operate as conventional units and follow special rules due to their size, mass, and inability to be stopped by smaller forces.

Titans are units that are slow, extremely resistant and very visible. They are not designed to play fine control of objectives, but to condition the space, break formations and force the enemy to react.

General Titan Rules

  • A Titan cannot capture or control Command points, unless a rule indicates otherwise.
  • During his movement, a Titan can traverse scenery elements such as buildings, ruins or large obstacles, as long as Don't end your move on them. A Titan cannot be placed, stopped, or remain on an element of scenery, nor use it as a surface, even if it can pass over it.
  • If during its movement a Titan passes over a unit of Line or Elite, said unit is automatically destroyed.
  • When a Titan is destroyed, a colossal explosion occurs. All units to 6” of the Titan suffer 1D6 automatic damage, which cannot be saved.

Unit profiles

Each ZeroLore unit has a profile that defines your capabilities on the battlefield.

This profile is used whenever the unit activates, attacks, or interacts with the scenario.

A drive profile contains the following values:

Class

Defines the functional role of the unit within the army.

Determines whether you can capture objectives, whether you can be a hero or leader, and what special rules you can apply based on your type (Line, Elite, Vehicle, Monster, or Hero).

Movement

Indicates the maximum distance, in inches (”), that the unit can move when performing a move action.

This value is also used to:

  • Dash
  • Withdrawal

Lives

Represents the total amount of damage the unit can take before being destroyed.

  • Each point of damage reduces the unit's Lives.
  • When Lives come to 0, the unit withdraws from the game as destroyed.

Save

Indicates the minimum necessary result in 1D6 to block a received impact.

Example:

A Save of 4+ blocks impacts with results of 4, 5 or 6.

Speed

It represents the reaction speed of the unit.

  • In close combat, the unit with the highest Speed act first, unless one has loaded.
  • Some abilities and doctrines modify this value temporarily.

Specialty

Includes special behaviors, design clarifications, or unique rules specific to the unit.

These rules apply whenever the unit is in play, unless otherwise noted.

Squads

Indicates the minimum and maximum number of thumbnails which can include the unit when playing in squads mode.

This value is not used in skirmish mode.

Value

It represents the cost in points of the unit.

  • It is used to balance confrontations.
  • Determines the size and composition of armies.
  • It is also used to calculate the score if there are several units in the same command point.

(suggested image: example of a unit sheet with each statistic indicated)

Weapon equipment

Unless a rule indicates otherwise, each unit can carry a single ranged weapon and a single melee weapon.

If a unit has more than one weapon of the same type, this must be explicitly indicated in its profile or special rules.

Ranged Weapon Profiles

Ranged weapons are described by their own profile.

Each weapon indicates how its attack resolves when used.

A ranged weapon profile includes:

Weapon name

Identify the weapon used.

Attacks

Number of dice rolled when making a ranged attack with this weapon.

Each die represents an independent projectile or shot.

Distance

Maximum weapon range in inches (").

The unit can only attack targets within this range.

Impacts

Minimum result needed on each die to hit.

  • A result of 6 always counts as a critical hit, unless a rule indicates otherwise.

Damage/Critical Damage

Indicate how many Lives are lost for each impact:

  • Damage: normal impact.
  • critical damage: hit obtained with a result of 6 or according to the weapon's ability.

Weapon skills

Special rules associated only with that weapon.

  • They only apply while the weapon is operational.
  • If the weapon is destroyed or silenced, these abilities cannot be used.

Extra value

Additional point cost for equipping this weapon, if applicable.

(suggested image: ranged weapon sheet broken down)

Close Combat Weapon Profiles

Melee Combat weapons follow a similar structure.

A melee weapon profile includes:

Weapon name

Identify the weapon used.

Attacks

Number of dice the unit rolls when attacking in close combat.

Damage/Critical Damage

They work the same as ranged weapons.

Skills

Special rules that only affect this type of attack or this specific weapon.

Extra value

Additional cost for equipping the weapon, if applicable.

(suggested image: melee weapon token with attacks and damage)

Modifiers

Some rules use modifiers expressed as +X or -X.

  • +X indicates that this value is added to the affected attribute or roll.
  • x indicates that this value is subtracted from the affected attribute or roll.

Examples:

  • +1 Attack die: Adds an additional die to the roll.
  • 1 to Hits value- The result needed to hit increases by 1.
  • +3 Speed: The unit acts as if it had 3 more Speed ​​points during that turn.

Modifiers only apply for the time indicated by the rule that grants them and they do not accumulate, unless otherwise indicated.

Weapon and unit skills

Skills may belong to:

  • The unit, or
  • A concrete weapon.
  • Unit abilities apply whenever the unit is in play.
  • Weapon skills only apply when that weapon is used.

Skills and their activation time are detailed in specific tables for easy reference during the game.

Weapon skills

Some weapons have special abilities which modify the way their attacks are resolved.

These skills:

  • They apply only while the weapon is operational.
  • They can affect the attack, the defender or the environment.
  • They are applied only at the indicated time.

If a unit loses or cannot use a weapon, cannot benefit of his abilities.

(suggested image: skill icons next to weapons)

When a target is hit by this weapon, loses +X to your Save against that attack.

Timing: Shooting

  • If the unit has moved, the weapon gets +1 to Hits value.
  • If the unit has not moved, the weapon gets -1 to Hits value.

Timing: Shooting

When the target is at half or less of the range of the weapon, this sum +X Attacks.

Timing: Shooting

4.Gunslinger

The unit can fire this weapon even if it is locked in melee combat, but only against the unit with which he fights in close combat.

Timing: Shooting

Timing: Shooting and melee

Allows reroll all failed attack rolls.

Command Doctrines

The Command Doctrines represent tactical orders, coordinated maneuvers, or strategic impulses that a commander can execute during battle.

The doctrines:

  • They are declared during the Initiative Phase.
  • They affect a unit or squad, unless otherwise indicated.
  • Its effects last the whole turn.
  • Each doctrine can only be used once per game.

The doctrines are public and must be clearly declared to the opponent before being applied.

(suggested image: cards or doctrine markers displayed next to the army)

List of Command Doctrines

1.Heavy Bash

The unit or squad engaged in close combat pushes 1” to his opponent, ending the fight.

2.Aimed Shot

The chosen unit or squad loses one action, but win +1 Attack die firing during this turn.

3.Frenzy

The chosen unit can use an additional move action this turn.

4.Agility in combat

The unit or squad wins +3 to your Speed during this turn.

5.Ours is the victory

The unit or squad wins +3 to its Value during this turn.

6.Immediate reaction

The player can activate two units or squads in a row during this turn.

7.Extra Prepared

The unit or squad wins an additional Ready action during this turn.

8.Perfect alignment

The unit ignores all enemy partial covers during this turn.

Any unit/squad that has a doctrine bonus can be marked with some type of token.

Conquest Mode (Official Battle Mode)

The Conquest Mode is the standard ZeroLore game format.

It represents balanced confrontations where both players compete for control of the battlefield by occupying strategic areas and destroying key units.

This mode serves as main reference for:

  • List creation.
  • Army design.
  • Competitive balance.

Game preparation

Before starting the game:

  1. The players agree on the size of the army.
  1. They place the Command points or control zones on the battlefield.
  1. The size, shape and position of the points is free, as long as both players agree.
  1. The deployment is carried out following the standard rules.

(suggested image: table with Command points placed)

Conquest Scoring

During the game, players earn points in the following ways:

Control of Command points

  • Every command point is worth 5 or 10 points, as agreed before the game.
  • The control is evaluated at the start of the turn, during the Initiative Phase.

Destruction of enemy units

Destroying an enemy unit earns the following points:

  • Unit of Line: 3 points
  • Unit of Elite: 5 points
  • Vehicle or Monster: 10 points

Secondary Missions

In each turn:

  • They are chosen 2 random side quests during the Initiative Phase.
  • It is possible to change missions once the turn has started.
  • Each completed side quest grants +5 points.

Secondary Missions

Deny progress

No enemy unit ends its turn within your half of the field.

Height gives me an advantage

You end your turn with a unit in an elevated position with no enemies at the same height.

Enemy advance intercepted

You make an attack (shooting or melee) against an enemy unit that moved this turn and deal damage to it.

Constant pressure

You end the turn with at least one enemy unit engaged in close combat.

Coordinated neutralization

Two of your units deal damage to the same enemy unit in the same turn.

Precise strike

One of your units deals damage only to the unit that activated just before you.

Express relocation

Two of your units make a move that takes them into cover this turn.

Bring down their leaders

You deal damage this turn to an enemy unit of Elite.

Secure the ground

You control any command point that was not under your control at the start of the turn.

Perfect advance

Two of your units/squads make a move and none takes damage during the turn.

Blind spot

An enemy unit/squad takes shot damage while partially hidden by cover.

Back to back

Two of your units/squads end the turn less than 2” each other and both have dealt damage this turn.

End of the Game

The game ends when:

  • The agreed end condition is reached, or
  • A player exceeds the established scoring threshold.

The player with the most points at the end of the game is the victor.

Closing Notes

ZeroLore is designed to be flexible, modular and adaptable.

This rulebook provides a solid foundation, but players are free to create additional scenarios, campaigns, and rules while maintaining the fundamental structure of the system.
